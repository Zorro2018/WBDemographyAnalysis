The Immigration Question — What NCRB and Intelligence Data Show

NCRB data (2017) reveals that West Bengal accounted for 49% of all criminal cases registered against foreigners across India, with 1,034 of 1,098 cases for violations of the Foreigners Act being against Bangladeshi nationals. IB sources cited by Swarajya reported that WB border districts — Malda, Murshidabad, Uttar Dinajpur, South 24 Parganas — are the primary settlement zones for illegal Bangladeshi immigrants, both Hindu and Muslim. [Source: Swarajya / NCRB 2017]

Mamata Government's Role: Shielding Illegal Immigrants from Identification

Swarajya and Organiser have consistently documented how the TMC government under Mamata Banerjee actively obstructed NRC implementation, refused to set up detention centres (despite approving them internally), and declared WB a "safe haven" for illegal immigrants — directly incentivising continued illegal entry. The Chief Minister's public statements opposing any identification exercise, combined with no-contest to Foreigners Tribunal proceedings in many cases, allowed large-scale document procurement (Aadhaar, voter IDs) by undocumented Bangladeshis. [Swarajya, Organiser, 2019–2025]

The "Chicken Neck" Security Dimension — A National Security Concern

The Siliguri Corridor, barely 22 km wide, connects India's seven northeastern states to the rest of the country. It passes directly through the Muslim-majority belt of Murshidabad–Malda–Uttar Dinajpur. CPS India analyst Dr. J.K. Bajaj noted: "It is indeed surprising that this turning of the native religions of Bharat into a minority in a crucial border region has happened in the last six decades of our independent polity." The 2025 Murshidabad riots prompted the West Bengal Governor to file a report with the Union MHA citing "rising radicalisation" in this zone. [Governor C.V. Ananda Bose report, May 2025; CPS India 2016]

Finding 1 — Accelerating Demographic Shift, Fastest Since 1971

CPS India (Dr. J.K. Bajaj, "Religion Data of Census 2011: West Bengal") documented that during 2001–11, the Muslim decadal growth rate in WB was 102% above that of Hindus — the fourth-highest growth gap in any Indian state after Assam, Kerala, and Uttarakhand. The shift has accelerated in each decade since 1971–81, the period when illegal immigration from Bangladesh began rising sharply following the 1971 war. The Hindu share fell from 78.4% to 70.5% in six decades; current projections (muslimsocieties.org / CPS India) place the Muslim share at ~29–30% by 2031 if the trend continues.

Finding 2 — Illegal Immigration Is a Documented Driver, Not a Myth

NCRB 2017 data showing WB accounting for 49% of India's Foreigners Act violations, a Union Home Ministry figure of 57 lakh illegal Bangladeshis in WB (2004), and IB intelligence (reported by Swarajya) confirming massing of illegal immigrants in border districts — taken together, this constitutes a documented evidentiary basis for immigration as a significant driver of the demographic shift in border districts. This is separate from the natural growth component, which also exists. The claim by left-leaning organisations that immigration is negligible is contradicted by government data and intelligence sources.

Finding 3 — Communal Violence Tracks the Demographic Belt with High Fidelity

Nearly all major communal incidents since 2010 — Deganga, Kaliachak, Dhulagarh, Baduria-Basirhat, Asansol, Ram Navami clashes 2023–24, and the Murshidabad Waqf riots 2025 — occurred in districts with Muslim populations above 30%. Murshidabad (66%) and Malda (~50%) are disproportionately represented. Districts with under 5% Muslim share (Bankura, Purulia) have zero documented communal incidents. The geographic correlation is not contested — the question is only of causation. The evidence strongly points to a combination of population density, political instrumentalization by TMC, and cross-border radicalization influence in border districts.

Finding 4 — The TMC Appeasement Architecture Enables Both Violence Types

Swarajya and Organiser have documented a systematic pattern: imam stipends (no Hindu priest equivalents); banning Hindu processions coinciding with Islamic events; obstruction of NRC; refusal to implement the Waqf Act but simultaneous encouraging of anti-Waqf protests; and a record of police inaction in communal incidents that disadvantages Hindus. This architecture has two consequences: it enables communal violence against Hindus in Muslim-majority areas while simultaneously fueling BJP's counter-mobilization — and the resulting cadre violence. Both forms of violence trace to the same political incentive structure.

Finding 5 — Political (Cadre) Violence Is Structurally Separate But Equally Unprecedented

West Bengal's political violence — the Left's 34-year cadre monopoly, the TMC's retributive violence in 2011, and the BJP–TMC clashes of 2019–21 — is driven by the "party-society" model and is not primarily communal in character. However, it shares the same enabling condition: a culture of total impunity enabled by state capture of the police. ORF notes that WB has averaged 20 political killings/year since 1999 — a figure with no parallel in any large Indian state. The 2022 Rampurhat massacre and chronic post-election displacement of BJP workers are the most recent manifestations of this deeply embedded pathology.

Finding 6 — The Hindu Displacement from Bangladesh and WB: A One-Way Flow

CPS India's most significant finding is the asymmetry: the Hindu share in Bangladesh fell by 13.6 percentage points (from 22.9% to 9.3%) between 1951 and 2011 — driven by documented persecution, forced conversions, land-grabs, and religious violence. Simultaneously, the Muslim share in WB rose 7.16 points. Persecuted Bangladeshi Hindus flee into WB (a large fraction of WB's Bangladesh-origin population is Hindu), while Muslim economic migrants and some persecuted Muslims also enter. The result is a net demographic shift in WB that reflects the failure of both the Bangladeshi state to protect its minorities and the Indian state to control its border.

The Long Decline: From India's Third-Largest Economy to a Persistent Laggard

The EAC-PM Working Paper on Relative Economic Performance (2024) documents one of the starkest economic deteriorations in post-Independence India: West Bengal held 10.5% of India's GDP in 1960-61, when it was the third-largest state economy — behind only Maharashtra. By 2023-24, that share had fallen to 5.6%, a near-halving in six decades. Per capita income, once above the national average in the 1960s, is now approximately 20% below it. The EAC-PM paper notes this as a "continuous decline throughout this period" with no reversal.

West Bengal's share of India's industrial output collapsed from 9.8% in 1980–81 to 5% by 1997–98 — a structural de-industrialisation that pre-dates the TMC and reflects Left Front labour policies. It has not recovered.

Root Causes: What the EAC-PM and NITI Aayog Found

The EAC-PM paper (2024) and NITI Aayog's 2025 macro-fiscal report identify multiple structural causes working across political regimes: labour militancy and capital flight during the 1970s–80s that permanently damaged industrial capacity; Left Front policy that prioritised land reform and rural patronage over manufacturing FDI; a credit-deposit gap 25 percentage points below the national average as of 2021, meaning savings generated in WB are not reinvested there; and high committed expenditure (76% of revenue on salaries, pensions, interest per CAG 2011) leaving minimal capital spend. BusinessToday (April 2026) notes that the decline spans all three governing parties — Congress, Left, TMC — making it structural rather than partisan.

The Demographic-Economic Nexus: A Compounding Trap

The economic decline and the demographic shift are analytically connected through several channels. First, Hindu middle-class and business community outmigration from WB to other states — accelerated by insecurity and economic stagnation — reduces the tax base and skilled workforce. Second, Muslim-majority border districts (Murshidabad, Malda, Uttar Dinajpur) are among WB's poorest and lowest-credit districts, meaning the demographic shift is concentrated in areas least able to generate organic growth. Third, political violence and communal instability drive away private investment — WB's FDI between October 2019 and June 2025 was just ₹15,256 crore, a fraction of comparable states like Gujarat or Tamil Nadu. The combination of political instability, demographic pressure, and economic hollowing creates a self-reinforcing cycle.

The "Paradox of Growth": Nominal GSDP Rises, Relative Position Falls

IBEF (2025) reports WB's GSDP at ₹20.3 lakh crore in 2025-26, growing at ~12% nominally. The state government points to these headline numbers as evidence of economic health. But the NITI Aayog and EAC-PM analysis shows this is largely a function of India-wide inflation and nominal price levels — in real constant-price terms, WB grew at only 4.3% per year between 2012 and 2022, well below the national 5.6%. The debt-to-GSDP ratio, while improving from 40.65% (2010-11) to ~37% (2022-23), remains above the median state. The state's fiscal deficit of 4.0% of GSDP exceeds the median Indian state. Per capita income at ₹141,373 (2022-23) is approximately 20% below the national average — a reversal from the 1960s when WB was above the national average.
